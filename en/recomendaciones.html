Section 7 — New

✅ Recommendations

Actionable guide based on scientific evidence to reduce AI's environmental impact — by actor type, time horizon, and impact level

The findings of this study have no full scientific value unless they translate into concrete actions. This section presents specific recommendations, verified in the literature, for the five types of actors who can accelerate the transition toward environmentally responsible AI. Recommendations are ordered by implementation horizon and expected impact level.

🏢7.1 For Tech Companies and Datacenter Operators
  • R1
    Publish complete environmental reports with standard methodology

    Adopt the GHG Protocol Scope 1–2–3 framework specifically for AI systems, reporting carbon per training and inference session, water consumption (direct and indirect), and e-waste generated. Generic corporate environmental reporting is not enough: AI models must have their own environmental line item.

    ⚡ Impact: Transparency + Regulation
  • R2
    Prioritize efficiency over scale as a success metric

    DeepSeek-R1 (January 2025) demonstrated it is possible to achieve performance comparable to GPT-4 with approximately 96% fewer computational resources. Companies must reorient their success metrics from parameter count ("the biggest model") toward the performance/emissions ratio. Internal benchmarks should include an environmental dimension.

    🌫️ Impact: -90% CO₂ per task
  • R3
    Relocate datacenters to zones with clean energy and abundant water

    This study's geographic analysis shows that the same infrastructure can have a carbon footprint up to 90× lower in Iceland or Norway than in Poland or India. Microsoft, Google, and Meta already operate centers in Northern Europe; the industry must expand this practice and avoid installing datacenters in water-stressed zones (AQUEDUCT score classification ≥4).

    ⚡ Potential reduction: up to 90× CO₂
  • R4
    Implement waterless or closed-loop cooling technologies

    Direct air cooling in cold climates (free-cooling), closed-loop water systems, and dielectric liquid immersion cooling can reduce water consumption by 95–99%. Microsoft launched its first "zero water" datacenter in August 2024. This technology must become the industry standard, not the exception.

    💧 Potential: -95% water consumption
  • R5
    Extend hardware lifespan and establish responsible recycling programs

    AI GPUs have 2–3 year replacement cycles, versus 5–7 years for conventional servers. Companies must establish lifespan-extension programs (refurbishing, downgrading to lower-consumption inference), and ensure discontinued hardware is recycled under R2/e-Stewards certifications — not exported to third countries without adequate recycling capacity.

    🗑️ Impact: toxic e-waste reduction
  • R6
    Adopt model efficiency techniques: quantization, distillation, pruning

    INT8/INT4 quantization, knowledge distillation, and pruning of inactive weights can reduce inference computational cost by 50–80% with minimal performance loss. Inference represents 80–90% of total AI compute; optimizing it has a greater environmental impact than optimizing training.

    ⚡ Inference: -50–80% energy
🏛️7.2 For Governments and Regulatory Bodies
  • R7
    Make AI environmental reporting mandatory, not voluntary

    The EU should expand the EU AI Act to include a mandatory environmental reporting section. The U.S. should reverse the weakening of environmental reviews for datacenters. A minimum international standard should require: CO₂ emissions per training session (Scope 1–3), Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE), and an e-waste management plan. Without mandatory requirements, self-reporting is insufficient.

    ⚖️ Horizon: short-term (1–2 years)
  • R8
    Create tax incentives linked to real environmental efficiency

    Subsidies and tax benefits for the AI industry should be conditioned on verifiable environmental metrics: PUE ≤1.2, 100% renewable energy within 5 years, WUE ≤0.5 L/kWh, and a certified e-waste management plan. Tax exemptions for datacenters without environmental conditions are wealth transfers that socialize ecological costs.

    💰 Instrument: green fiscal policy
  • R9
    Regulate critical mineral extraction in the AI supply chain

    Consumer governments must implement supply-chain due-diligence laws for critical minerals (cobalt, lithium, rare earths, gallium) similar to the EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive. Tech companies that purchase chips must be able to certify that minerals do not come from zones with child labor, aquifer destruction, or violations of indigenous communities' rights.

    ⛏️ Impact: fair supply chain
  • R10
    Ban datacenter installation in severely water-stressed zones

    Governments in water-scarce regions (Chile, inland Spain, India, the Middle East, southwestern U.S.) must establish moratoria or bans on AI datacenter installation until they have cooling technology with no net water consumption. The Los Angeles incidents (January 2025) — hydrants without pressure during active fires — illustrate the direct human cost of not doing so.

    💧 Urgent in: CA, Chile, Spain, India
  • R11
    Fund public research in efficient AI and standard metrics

    Governments must fund research lines in standard AI environmental impact methodologies (which this paper contributes to developing), computational efficiency techniques, and next-generation chip design with reduced manufacturing impact. "Green AI" research should not depend exclusively on companies with direct commercial interests.

    🔬 Horizon: medium-term
🔬7.3 For Researchers and the Scientific Community
  • R12
    Systematically report environmental cost in new model publications

    Every scientific publication presenting a new AI model must include, as a mandatory peer-reviewed section, the report of its training carbon footprint, water consumption, and hardware used. PNAS, Nature, Science, and NeurIPS journals must adopt this policy. Without this norm, the scientific community perpetuates the opacity it criticizes in industry.

    📄 Immediate action for authors
  • R13
    Develop standard methodology for the environmental impact of inference

    The largest scientific gap identified in this study is the absence of a validated methodology to quantify the environmental impact of inference (daily model use), which represents 80–90% of AI compute. Update: the public calculator accompanying this paper already incorporates an inference module based on real measurements from Jegham et al. (arXiv:2505.09598, 2025) — a first step toward closing this gap. However, broader systematic academic studies are needed covering: energy per query type at larger scale, water footprint per generated token, and geographic variation of impact from distributed inference.

    🔭 Critical research gap
  • R14
    Prioritize reproducibility and open access in environmental data

    Measurement tools, per-model energy consumption datasets, and calculation frameworks must be published under open licenses (CC BY / Apache 2.0). The carbon footprint calculator developed alongside this paper is an example of commitment to open science. Publicly funded research in sustainable AI should be publicly accessible.

    🌐 Committed: this paper
  • R15
    Study differential impacts in the Global South

    Current research on AI environmental impact concentrates in the Global North. Field studies are needed in mining communities in Congo, Bolivia, and Chile; in e-waste dumps in Ghana and Bangladesh; and in ecosystems affected by datacenters in India and Southeast Asia. Environmental justice requires that research include those who bear the costs of global AI.

    ⚖️ Environmental and academic justice
👤7.4 For Individual Users and Organizations
  • R16
    Prefer efficient models for tasks that don't require the largest model

    Using GPT-4 to draft a simple email has the same environmental cost as for a complex analysis — but the complex analysis is the only task that justifies that cost. Users should calibrate model power to task complexity: 7B–13B parameter models solve 80% of everyday use cases with a fraction of the environmental impact.

    ⚡ Action: today
  • R17
    Demand environmental transparency as a vendor selection criterion

    Organizations that contract AI services (APIs, platforms, consulting) should include environmental transparency as an evaluation criterion: does the provider publish its PUE? Does it report water consumption? Does it have a certified e-waste management plan? Market pressure — especially from large companies with ESG commitments — can accelerate changes that regulation takes time to implement.

    🏢 For procurement and ESG teams
  • R18
    Calculate and report AI carbon footprint in corporate sustainability reports

    Companies using AI models in production must include Scope 3 emissions derived from AI use in their GHG Protocol reports. This paper's interactive calculator (lizzy0981.github.io/calculadora-huella-carbono-ia/) can be the starting point for this calculation. Carbon accounting that excludes AI use is incomplete and underestimates the real impact.

    📊 Resource: public calculator
🌍7.5 For the Global South and Developing Countries
  • R19
    Establish sovereign terms for critical mineral extraction

    Global South countries that hold deposits of cobalt (DRC), lithium (Bolivia, Chile, Argentina), and rare earths (China, Brazil) must negotiate extraction agreements that include: technology transfer, on-site processing (not exporting raw ore), verified environmental restoration funds, and indigenous community participation in exploitation decisions.

    ⛏️ Natural resource sovereignty
  • R20
    Reject AI e-waste disguised as "donation" or "technological aid"

    Dumping obsolete AI hardware in the form of "technological donations" to countries without adequate recycling capacity transfers toxic costs to the Global South. Recipient countries must establish certification requirements for importing used hardware and reject equipment whose end-of-life management is not funded by the exporter under international standards.

    🗑️ Environmental and health protection
  • R21
    Invest in efficient local AI, not dependence on Global North mass models

    Global South countries must prioritize the development and deployment of small, efficient AI models (SLMs) adapted to their languages and realities, trained with local renewable energy. Dependence on massive model APIs externalized in the North implies constant currency transfer, without developing local capabilities. AI access should not reproduce dynamics of technological dependence.

    🌐 Technological sovereignty

The Path Exists — Will Is the Variable

None of this paper's recommendations require sacrificing AI progress. Scientific evidence shows that efficient models like DeepSeek-R1 or Claude Sonnet can do the work of much larger systems at a fraction of the environmental cost. The challenge is not technological: it is one of incentives, transparency, and political will. AI can be the engine of 21st-century human development without needing to become one of its main ecological threats.

"AI sustainability is not a constraint on progress — it is its long-term condition of possibility."
