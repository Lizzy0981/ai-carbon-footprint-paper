Interpretation of findings, implications for industry and science, comparison with existing literature, and future research directions

6.1AI as a Multidimensional Environmental Problem: A Paradigm Shift

The results of this study, integrated with the most recent research (2023–2025), confirm that the environmental impact of artificial intelligence can no longer be evaluated solely through the lens of carbon. Accumulated evidence from Strubell et al.'s foundational work (2019) to IEA reports (2025) and Nature Sustainability (Xiao et al., 2025) establishes a multidimensional picture that demands a transformation in how industry, regulators, and society evaluate the real cost of AI.

❌ Previous Paradigm

AI's environmental impact is measured exclusively in CO₂ emissions during training

↓
✅ New Scientific Framework

CO₂ + water + e-waste + minerals + biodiversity + inference + full supply chain

❌ Previous View

AI models are "software" — their impact is primarily energetic and transitory

↓
✅ Documented Reality

AI requires massive physical infrastructure with permanent environmental impacts on water, soil, and ecosystems

❌ Previous Assumption

Larger model size always implies greater value; scale is a virtue without cost

↓
✅ Scientific Evidence

R²=0.89 confirms size-emissions correlation; efficient models like DeepSeek-R1 achieve similar performance with 5–10x fewer resources

Original Contribution to the Scientific Field

No study published to date combines the five pillars (carbon, water, e-waste, minerals, biodiversity) in a single methodological framework with empirical validation and an interactive public calculation tool. 6.3Implications for the Global Technology Industry

The findings of this study have direct and concrete consequences for companies that develop and deploy AI models. The 100x difference in carbon footprint between GPT-4 (1,820,000 kg CO₂) and Claude Sonnet (18,200 kg CO₂) for models of comparable performance demonstrates that environmental efficiency is not a goal at odds with technical performance — it is a deliberate design choice. Emerging data on water and minerals reinforce this conclusion.

The Race for the "Biggest Model" Is Environmentally Unsustainable

Analysis of the 2018–2024 period shows that training energy consumption has grown approximately 300,000x from AlexNet (2012) to GPT-4 (2023). Without changes in design architecture, the IEA's projection that datacenter electricity consumption will exceed Japan's total demand by 2030 is inevitably conservative. DeepSeek-R1 (January 2025) demonstrated that models with performance comparable to GPT-4o can be trained with ~96% fewer computational resources, suggesting that the industry has prioritized scale over efficiency for marketing reasons, not technical necessity.

The revelation of water impact —148 million liters daily estimated for ChatGPT alone— fundamentally changes the business argument. Companies that locate datacenters in water-stressed zones to take advantage of low land and energy costs are externalizing a cost that falls on local communities and ecosystems that have no representation in corporate decisions. This pattern, documented in Chile, Spain, India, and the southwestern U.S., constitutes a new form of environmental liability that regulatory frameworks have not yet addressed.

Positive Signals on the Horizon

Google published in 2025 the first detailed methodology to measure water and carbon per inference query in Gemini, setting a transparency precedent. Microsoft launched its first operational "zero water" datacenter in August 2024. UNEA-7 included datacenter environmental transparency on its December 2025 agenda. Cornell and other universities have published detailed "roadmaps" demonstrating that 73% reductions in carbon and 86% in water are technically achievable with combined location, efficiency, and clean energy strategies.

6.5Study Limitations

Scientific integrity requires explicitly acknowledging the methodological limitations that affect the precision of the presented results. These limitations do not invalidate the general conclusions, but rather define the margins of uncertainty within which they should be interpreted:

  • 1
    Industry Opacity

    The main model developers (OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Meta) do not publish complete technical data on hardware used, number of GPUs, exact training duration, or specific PUE factors. This study's estimates are based on available public data, inferences from technical publications, and cross-validation with environmental reports. As disclosure improves, calculated values can be refined.

  • 2
    Inference Is 80–90% of the Real Problem

    This study focuses primarily on training, which is the most measurable phase. However, inference (daily model use) represents 80–90% of AI's total energy consumption according to multiple sources. The methodology for calculating inference is more complex and available data scarcer; this is an acknowledged limitation that defines the future research agenda.

  • 3
    Variability in Water Impact Data

    Water consumption values per query vary up to 100x among different sources and methodologies (5 mL vs 519 mL per query). These differences reflect different measurement scopes (direct cooling only vs. full lifecycle), model generations, datacenter types, and geographic locations. This study reports ranges, not single values, to honestly reflect this uncertainty.

  • 4
    Pace of Technological Change

    AI models evolve in weekly cycles. Hardware and efficiency data that are accurate today may be partially obsolete within months. The emergence of MoE architectures, distilled models, and improved-efficiency chips (such as NVIDIA's Blackwell) continually alters the landscape. Annual review of the interactive calculator's data is recommended.

  • 5
    Supply Chain: Incomplete Data

    The environmental impact of critical mineral extraction and processing lacks standardized and comparable data. Mining companies and chip manufacturers rarely publish full lifecycle data with transparent methodology. Baotou-type data comes from occasional academic research, not systematic industry reports.

6.6Future Research Directions

The identified limitations directly define research priorities in sustainable AI for the coming years. This field needs:

📐
Standard Methodology for Inference

Develop and validate a formula analogous to the training one to estimate inference impact by query type, model, and cloud provider.

💧
Per-Model Water Audit

Establish protocols for AI providers to report water consumption per query under standardized methodology (direct + indirect + manufacturing).

🌍
Impact on Local Ecosystems

Field studies in communities affected by datacenters: Chile, Spain, India, Netherlands, Virginia. Quantification of impacts on aquifers, biodiversity, and local temperature.

🔄
Full Chip Lifecycle

Standardized lifecycle analysis (LCA) for AI GPUs including manufacturing, operation, recycling, and final disposal, with verified manufacturer data.

🤖
Efficient AI: Architecture Correlations

Expand correlation analysis to include MoE architectures, quantized models, and edge AI, establishing emission reduction factors per optimization technique.

⚖️
Environmental Justice and AI

Quantify the unequal distribution of AI's benefits vs. environmental costs between the Global North (benefits) and the Global South (extractive and e-waste costs).

Discussion synthesis: This paper integrates for the first time the five vectors of AI's environmental impact (carbon, water, e-waste, minerals, biodiversity) into a single scientific framework with empirical validation and a public tool. The 100x difference between similar models confirms that environmental efficiency is possible and depends on design decisions. The absence of complete data on inference, water, and supply chain is not a scientific limitation — it is an industry transparency failure that research and regulation must correct.
