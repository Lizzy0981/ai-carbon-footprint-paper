Section 5 — New ⛏️

Critical Minerals, Ecosystems, and Biodiversity

AI does not exist in thin air — its chips and servers require metals extracted from deep within the earth, at an environmental and human cost rarely made visible

Manufacturing a 2 kg computer requires 800 kg of raw materials. The microchips that power AI models depend on rare earth elements extracted under environmentally destructive conditions, often in regions with weak or nonexistent environmental regulations. China controls 60–99% of the refining of the most critical minerals for AI, and sector expansion could trigger an escalation in seabed mining of practically unexplored ecosystems.

5.1 Critical Minerals Required by AI

The minerals required for AI infrastructure range from common metals to highly specialized rare earth elements. Each GPU, server, and cooling system contains dozens of these materials, whose supply chain generates environmental impacts from mine to landfill:

⚡
Cobalt (Co)
Backup batteries in datacenters

~70% of global production comes from the DRC (Democratic Republic of the Congo), where artisanal mining involves child labor and contaminates local rivers. Extraction destroys tropical forests and directly affects indigenous communities.

DRC: 70% global production — IEA 2024
🔋
Lithium (Li)
Batteries and storage systems

Extraction from Bolivia's Salar (the world's largest deposit) uses enormous volumes of water in already arid regions, threatening unique ecosystems like Uyuni lagoon. Indigenous communities report consultations that are "merely performative."

Bolivia: largest global reserve — affects unique ecosystems
🔲
Gallium (Ga)
Chips and power electronics for GPUs

China controls 99% of global gallium refining. A lifecycle study in Shanxi identifies the sodium hydroxide used in its refining as the main cause of water toxicity. The IEA projects demand will exceed 10% of current supply by 2030.

China: 99% global refining — IEA Energy & AI (2025)
🧲
Neodymium (Nd)
Permanent magnets in GPU motors and fans

A rare earth element essential for the powerful magnets in cooling system motors. Its extraction in China has generated massive environmental liabilities, including the toxic lake of Baotou, Inner Mongolia.

Baotou: 180 Mt toxic waste lake
💎
Copper (Cu)
Cables, heat sinks, PCBs

The U.S. imports ~37% of its copper. New mines require 15–20 years from discovery to production. Copper mining in northern Chile faces protests from indigenous communities over water use in arid zones.

U.S. imports 37% — FP Analytics (2025)
🔩
Ultrapure Silicon (Si)
Base substrate of all semiconductors

Although silicon is abundant, purifying it to semiconductor grade (99.9999999% purity) requires enormously energy- and water-intensive processes. Chip factories (fabs) are concentrated in Taiwan, South Korea, and the U.S.

Purification: industry's most water-intensive process
🏭 Case Study: Baotou, Inner Mongolia, China

In Baotou, the world's center for rare earth refining, the mineral extraction and processing has created a toxic lake spanning more than 5 square miles, containing more than 180 million tons of residual dust from mineral processing.

This lake contains radioactive thorium, sulfuric acid, and dozens of other contaminants that leach into local aquifers and surrounding agricultural soil. Nearby villages report elevated rates of cancer, osteoporosis, and congenital deformities. The ecosystem within a radius of kilometers has been irreversibly altered.

This is not an isolated case: it is the documented consequence of global demand for rare earth minerals for technology — including AI.

5.2 Impact on Biodiversity and Ecosystems

A study published in Carbon Neutral Systems (Springer Nature, December 2025) develops the first integrated "resource nexus" framework that analyzes AI's impacts on energy, water, materials, and land simultaneously. It identifies that biodiversity impacts are systemic, interconnected, and spatially unequal, with benefits concentrated in the Global North and ecological costs externalized to the Global South.

🌳
Habitat Destruction from Mining

Mining and smelting of critical minerals involves habitat destruction, introduction of invasive species via mining road networks, and long-term toxic effects on adjacent aquatic ecosystems.
Source: Springer Nature (2025)

🌱
Land Conversion for Datacenters

Large server farms occupy extensive areas, displacing local flora and fauna. A datacenter in Oregon spans 327 acres — more than 250 soccer fields. Its development in peri-urban areas disrupts ecological corridors and contributes to urban heat islands.
Source: Medium/Eastgate (2024)

🐟
Water Contamination

Leachate from e-waste landfills and mineral refining waste contaminate rivers, lakes, and aquifers. Mercury bioaccumulates in the aquatic food chain, affecting fish, birds, and marine mammals.
Source: Wang et al., Nature Comp. Sci. (2024)

🦜
Habitat Fragmentation

Road networks for mining access fragment previously continuous habitats, interrupting mammal and bird migration patterns, and facilitating poaching and illegal extraction of natural resources.
Source: Springer Nature Carbon Neutral Systems (2025)

💧
Competition for Water with Ecosystems

Datacenters in arid zones extract water from the same aquifers that supply wetlands and rivers critical for migratory birds and endemic wildlife. The southwestern U.S., Spain, and northern Chile are documented cases of active conflict.
Source: Lincoln Institute / IE Insights (2025)

🌡️
Heat Islands and Microclimate

Megadatacenters emit waste heat that raises local temperature by several degrees, altering microclimates that sustain sensitive ecosystems. In dense zones like Northern Virginia, with 300+ datacenters, the effect is regional and documented.
Source: Lincoln Institute (2025)

⚓ The Most Dangerous Frontier: Deep-Sea Mining

The explosive demand for AI metals is accelerating proposals for seabed mining — extracting metal nodules from the ocean floor in practically unexplored areas. The Clarion-Clipperton Zone, between Mexico and Hawaii, concentrates the greatest interest of U.S. and European mining companies.

The problem: these nodules rest in unique deep-water ecosystems that host uncatalogued species, many with critical roles in ocean biogeochemical cycles. Dredging or vacuuming the seafloor to recover these nodules irreversibly destroys habitats that took millions of years to form. This zone is located in international waters and its regulation is actively debated at the International Seabed Authority.

No adequate regulatory framework yet exists to assess or mitigate this impact before extraction begins.

5.3 Geopolitical Concentration of the Supply Chain

AI's dependence on critical minerals is geographically concentrated in a handful of countries, creating geopolitical vulnerabilities and perpetuating extractive models that externalize environmental cost toward regions with less bargaining power. The IEA reports that gallium demand could exceed 10% of current global supply by 2030.

🇨🇳
China

60%+ rare earth production. 99% gallium refining. De facto monopoly on processed REEs.

🇨🇩
DRC

70% of global cobalt. Questioned labor conditions. Active deforestation in mining zones.

🇧🇴
Bolivia / Chile / Argentina

World's largest lithium reserve ("Lithium Triangle"). Documented conflicts with indigenous communities.

🇲🇲
Myanmar

Major REE and tungsten producer. Illegal mining linked to armed conflict financing.

🇹🇼
Taiwan

TSMC produces 92% of the world's most advanced chips. High water dependency for manufacturing.

Global Inequity: Costs Are Not Shared
📊 Market Concentration — % of Global Control by Country

The Springer Nature (2025) study demonstrates that AI's benefits are concentrated in the Global North (U.S., Europe, China), while ecological costs — mining pollution, informal e-waste, habitat destruction — are externalized to the Global South: Sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America, and Southeast Asia. This is a form of digital environmental colonialism that the international regulatory framework has not adequately addressed.
