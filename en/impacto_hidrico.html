Water Impact of AI

The silent crisis: how artificial intelligence models consume, evaporate, and compete for the planet's most scarce resource

While public debate on AI and the environment has focused primarily on CO₂ emissions, a parallel crisis remains almost invisible: the massive consumption of fresh drinking water by data centers. In 2023, just training GPT-3 at Microsoft's centers directly evaporated 700,000 liters of fresh water — enough to supply 1,400 people for one day. And that's just one model, two years ago.

3.1 How Data Centers Consume Water

Water is not just an incidental byproduct of AI — it is a critical operational input without which cooling systems would fail within minutes. The energy demand of modern servers generates massive heat: an NVIDIA H100 GPU dissipates up to 700W of continuous thermal heat. At datacenter scale, with tens of thousands of GPUs operating in parallel, this heat must be constantly removed.

🌊
Evaporative Cooling

The most common method: fresh water circulates absorbing heat and then evaporates in cooling towers. The water does not return to the local water system — it is lost to the atmosphere.

~1.9 L water per kWh consumed (global WUE average)
⚡
Indirect Water (Electricity Generation)

Thermoelectric plants that supply electricity to datacenters require water for steam production and cooling. In the U.S., electricity generation withdraws 43.8 L and consumes 3.1 L per kWh.

U.S. indirect footprint: ~800 billion L/year
🏭
Chip Manufacturing

Semiconductors require ultrapure water in massive quantities during su fabricación. Apple reporta que el 99% de su huella hídrica total proviene de su cadena de suministro, no de sus operaciones directas.

Low recycling rate; potential for toxic waste
🏗️
Infrastructure Construction

Every new datacenter requires water during construction and its de paisajismo y climatización. Los proyectos de expansión en Virginia, Texas y Nevada absorben recursos hídricos de comunidades locales.

Texas: 49 billion gal/year in 2025 (HARC/Univ. Houston)

3.3 Geographic Crises: Datacenters in Water-Stressed Zones

One of the most critical aspects of AI's water impact is that nearly la mitad de los más de 9,000 datacenters del mundo se encuentran en regiones de alto estrés hídrico (Morgan Stanley Research, 2024). Esto significa que estos sistemas compiten directamente con comunidades humanas, agricultura y ecosistemas por el mismo recurso escaso. Las siguientes situaciones documentadas ilustran la escala del problema:

🇨🇱
Santiago, Chile

Google's datacenter in Cerrillos consumes ~7.6 million liters of drinking water daily in a region already suffering severe drought. It has faced litigation from local communities.
Source: Chile court filings (2024)

🇪🇸
Aragón, Spain

Amazon requested a 48% increase in water permits for its instalaciones en Aragón en 2024. Protestas bajo el lema "Tu nube está secando mi río".
Source: IE Insights / multiple media (2025)

🇺🇸
Texas, USA

Texas datacenters will consume 49 billion gallons of water in 2025 and could reach 399 billion by 2030 — equivalent to draining Lake Mead by 16 feet in one year.
Source: HARC / University of Houston (2025)

🇬🇧
United Kingdom

The government projects a water deficit of 5 billion liters daily by 2050. PM Starmer's first "AI Growth Zone" was planned in Culham, Oxfordshire, England's highest water-stress zone.
Source: UK government report (2025)

🇳🇱
Netherlands

Protests in Amsterdam and surrounding areas against the construction of mega-datacenters on agricultural land. The Dutch government implemented temporary restrictions on new construction permits.
Source: multiple European media (2023–2024)

🌍
Latin America (context)

The Caribbean and Central America are among the regions of the world with the highest projected water stress from climate change for 2030–2050. Unregulated AI expansion would deepen this crisis.
Source: IPCC AR6 (2022) / UN-Water (2023)

Critical Fact: AI's Water is Drinking Water

Unlike water used in agriculture or industry (which can be recycled or non-potable water), most datacenters require municipal-grade drinking water for their cooling systems, because impurities corrode equipment. This means AI competes directly for the same water humans drink. In regions with active drought — such as the southwestern U.S., northern Chile, and the Mediterranean — this competition becomes a concrete humanitarian emergency.

3.4 2025–2030 Projections: An Alarming Trend

Data from Cornell University researchers published in Nature Sustainability (November 2025) project that AI server deployment in the U.S. could drain between 731 and 1,125 million cubic meters of water annually between 2024 and 2030 — equivalent to the annual domestic consumption of 6 to 10 million Americans.

The study by de Vries-Gao published in Cell Reports Sustainability (December 2025) establishes that the global water footprint of AI systems could reach between 312.5 and 764.6 billion liters in 2025, equivalent to global annual bottled water consumption. This figure represents directly consumed water — not merely withdrawn — meaning it does not return to the local water cycle.

3.5 Technological and Water Policy Solutions

Although the outlook is concerning, both technological solutions and policy instruments exist that can significantly reduce AI's water impact. Combined implementation could achieve reductions of up to 86% in water consumption while maintaining the same processing capacity:

🧊
Direct Liquid Cooling (Immersion Cooling)

Submerges components in non-conductive liquid. Virtually eliminates the need for evaporative water. Microsoft launched its first "zero water" datacenter in August 2024.

📍
Strategic Location

Building datacenters in cold-climate regions (Iceland, Norway, northern Canada) drastically reduces the need for active cooling. Iceland uses geothermal energy and outside-air cooling.

♻️
Recycled and Non-Potable Water

Amazon, Meta, and Apple have begun using treated municipal water (reclaimed water) instead of fresh drinking water. This can reduce drinking water use by 50–70% when combined with other measures.

📊
Disclosure and Regulation

There is currently no legal requirement to report water consumption per datacenter in most countries. UNEA-7 (Nairobi, Dec. 2025) included datacenter water transparency as a priority agenda item.

🌬️
Adiabatic Cooling

Microsoft uses adiabatic cooling (outside air) when temperatures are below 29.4°C. In temperate and cold climates this covers most of the year, eliminating water use.

🤖
AI Efficient by Design

Architectures like MoE (Mixture of Experts), model distillation, and quantization reduce the compute required per inference. DeepSeek-R1 demonstrated in 2025 that highly efficient models require 5–10x fewer resources than their unoptimized equivalents.
