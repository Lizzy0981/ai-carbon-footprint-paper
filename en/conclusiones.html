Section 8 — Conclusions
🏁

Conclusions

Synthesis of findings, original contributions to the scientific field, and a call to action for environmentally responsible AI

This work set out two simultaneous goals: to build the first validated methodological framework to measure the carbon footprint of AI models, and to expand that framework toward a comprehensive vision of environmental impact that goes beyond CO₂. Both goals have been achieved. The conclusions that follow synthesize the most significant findings and their implications for the future of sustainable AI.

8.1Main Conclusions
  • C1
    Model scale is the most reliable predictor of carbon emissions

    Analysis of 30+ models confirms a statistically significant correlation between parameter count and CO₂ emissions (R²=0.89, p<0.001), following a power-law relationship. This finding establishes that model size, more than any other technical factor, determines its initial climate impact.

    R²=0.89 · p<0.001 · 30+ models
  • C2
    Geography can reduce impact up to 90× — location regulation is urgent

    Datacenter location is the single most impactful environmental factor identified in this study. Training the same model in Iceland vs. Poland produces a 90× difference in carbon emissions, with comparable variations in water consumption depending on water availability and available cooling technologies.

    Iceland 0.012 vs Poland 1.085 kg CO₂/kWh
  • C3
    Efficient models achieve comparable performance with a fraction of the impact

    The 100× difference in carbon footprint between GPT-4 (1,820,000 kg CO₂) and Claude Sonnet (18,200 kg CO₂) for equivalent tasks demonstrates that environmental efficiency is a design choice, not a technical compromise. DeepSeek-R1 (January 2025) reaffirmed this conclusion by achieving performance comparable to GPT-4o with approximately 96% fewer resources.

    100× difference · same functional capabilities
  • C4
    Water impact is as serious as climate impact — and less known

    Data expansion toward water impact reveals a documented but underestimated crisis: GPT-3 training evaporated 700,000 liters of drinking water; AI's global water footprint reached 312–764 billion liters in 2025. Social conflicts in Chile, Spain, Texas, and the UK demonstrate this is not a future projection — it is already a present reality.

    700,000 L GPT-3 · 312–764 billion L global (2025)
  • C5
    AI e-waste could become an unprecedented toxic crisis by 2030

    The 2–3 year lifespan of AI GPUs, combined with the industry's pace of expansion, projects the generation of between 1.2 and 5 million metric tons of electronic waste by 2030 — containing lead, mercury, arsenic, cadmium, and other persistent contaminants. With only 22% of global e-waste formally recycled, the no-intervention scenario is severe.

    1.2–5 Mt by 2030 · 2–3 year lifespan
  • C6
    The mineral supply chain externalizes ecological costs to the Global South

    Manufacturing a 2 kg computer requires 800 kg of raw materials. Critical minerals for AI (cobalt, lithium, rare earths, gallium) are predominantly extracted in Global South countries under conditions that include child labor, aquifer destruction, and ecosystem devastation. AI's benefits are concentrated in the Global North while ecological costs are externalized to the South: this asymmetry is ethically unsustainable and documents a growing environmental debt.

    800 kg raw materials per kg of hardware
  • C7
    Inference is 80–90% of the real environmental problem — and remains poorly measured

    Scientific literature, including this work, has focused on training because it is the most measurable phase. However, inference — the daily use of models by millions of people — represents 80–90% of AI's total compute according to the most recent data (LBNL 2024, IEA 2025). Measuring and reporting inference impact is the field's main pending agenda.

    80–90% of total compute is inference

8.2Original Contributions to the Scientific Field
📐
Validated Public Methodology

First validated formula (R²=0.89, error <6%) to estimate the carbon footprint of any AI model, publicly available under CC BY 4.0.

🗄️
Dataset of 30+ Models

First comparative carbon footprint database of AI models including LLMs, vision models, and specialized models with cross-validation.

💧
Integration of Water Impact

First integration of water impact alongside carbon in the same methodological framework with verified 2023–2025 data.

🔄
5-Dimension Framework

First paper to integrate CO₂, water, e-waste, minerals, and biodiversity into a single cohesive scientific framework with empirical evidence for each dimension.

⚡
Public Interactive Calculator — 3 Modules

Tool accessible at https://lizzy0981.github.io/calculadora-huella-carbono-ia/ with Training, Inference (Jegham et al. 2025), and Carbon ROI Calculator modules, in 11 languages, allowing anyone to estimate the environmental and financial impact of any AI model.

🌱
Sustainable AI Is Not Optional

By 2030, AI will consume electricity equivalent to Japan's entire demand, water equivalent to global bottled water consumption, and will generate millions of tons of toxic electronic waste. This paper demonstrates that the problem is quantifiable, the factors are identifiable, and solutions exist. What is missing is transparency, regulation, and the will to act. The AI you choose to use, design, and regulate has a real environmental cost. It is time to measure it, report it, and reduce it.
