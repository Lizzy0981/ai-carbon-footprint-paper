Introduction

Context, Problem, and Objectives

Artificial intelligence as a driver of climate change: from enthusiasm without metrics to scientific rigor

In 2023, training GPT-4 consumed an estimated equivalent of the energy used by 1,000 U.S. households in a year. In 2024, global data centers consumed 415 TWh of electricity — more than all of Argentina. By 2030, they will consume 945 TWh, equivalent to Japan's entire electricity demand. Despite these figures, no comprehensive, accessible, and public scientific framework existed — until this paper — to measure, compare, and communicate the environmental impact of AI models.

0.1Problem Context

Generative artificial intelligence has undergone unprecedented expansion since 2020, with models whose size multiplies every 12–18 months and whose global-scale deployment involves massive physical infrastructure: thousands of data centers, tens of millions of specialized GPUs, and a consumption of resources — energy, water, minerals — that exceeds by orders of magnitude what public discourse has acknowledged.

The foundational work of Strubell et al. (2019) first warned that training large natural language processing models carries significant "energy and policy costs." Since then, the field has evolved rapidly but in a fragmented way: some studies measure carbon, others water, others e-waste — without methodological integration or public tools that allow researchers, journalists, policymakers, and citizens to calculate and compare the real impact of the AI systems they use daily.

0.2Field Evolution: Scientific Timeline
2019
Strubell et al. — The Initial Alarm

First systematic quantification of the energy cost of training large NLP models. Estimates that training a transformer with hyperparameter search emits ~626,000 lbs of CO₂ — equivalent to five cars over their full lifetime.

ACL 2019 · Fundacional
2021–22
Patterson, Luccioni et al. — Methodological Deepening

Google and Hugging Face refine the methodologies. Patterson demonstrates that datacenter geographic location can reduce emissions up to 40×. Luccioni develops the first carbon audit for BLOOM (176B parameters).

Google / Hugging Face · Metodológico
2023
Li et al. — The Water Crisis Emerges

University of California Riverside discovers that GPT-3 evaporated 700,000 liters of drinking water during training. First systematic quantification of the water footprint of AI models.

UC Riverside · arXiv:2304.03271
2024
Wang et al. — E-Waste Enters the Debate

Nature Computational Science publishes the first systematic projection of e-waste generated by generative AI: between 1.2 and 5 million metric tons by 2030, with a catalog of associated toxins.

Nature Comp. Sci. · 2024
2025
IEA, Nature Sustainability, Cell Reports — Global Convergence

The International Energy Agency publishes "Energy and AI" (April 2025), the most authoritative analysis to date. Simultaneously, Xiao et al. (Cornell/Nature Sustainability) and de Vries-Gao (Cell Reports Sustainability) publish the most comprehensive quantitative frameworks for water and carbon.

IEA · Cornell · VU Amsterdam · 2025
0.3Study Objectives
O1
Develop a Replicable Methodology

Create a validated and replicable formula to calculate the carbon footprint of any AI model from publicly available technical specifications.

O2
Comparative Analysis of 30+ Models

Apply the methodology to the most relevant models on the market and publish the results publicly and free of charge.

O3
Comprehensive Environmental Framework

Expand the analysis beyond carbon to five dimensions: energy, water, waste, minerals, and biodiversity.

O4
Public Interactive Tool

Develop and publish an AI carbon footprint calculator with 3 modules — training, inference, and green ROI — accessible to any researcher, company, or citizen.

O5
Actionable Recommendations

Produce a framework of specific recommendations for companies, governments, researchers, and users to reduce the environmental impact of AI.
